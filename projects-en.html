Sound Design Projects

Reconstructed Opening Sequence Sound Design

Complete sound redesign of the film’s opening sequence.

All audio elements were recreated in DaVinci Resolve (Fairlight), with the aim of reinterpreting the emotional tension through sound.

The project is based on the concept of added value and Michel Chion’s theory of sound temporalization. In particular, it explores three main processes: temporal animation, linearization, and vectorization, used to shape the perception of time and the emotional construction of the scene.

Temporal Perception Study

This project explores the relationship between sound and the perception of time, aiming to avoid a static temporal reading of the scene./p>

The sound design is built to create a fluid and active perception of time, where motion exists not only in rhythm but also within the internal structure of sound.

Special attention is given to visual and sonic micro-rhythms, which interact to generate a dense, continuous, and non-linear temporality. Sound does not simply accompany the image, but actively reshapes its temporal perception through density and texture.

Non-Conventional Sound Materials

Sound redesign of the coconut-breaking scene in a stylized direction inspired by video game aesthetics.

The project uses synchresis as a guiding principle to build a hybrid sound system where realistic and artificial elements coexist coherently with the image.

The goal is to demonstrate that sound credibility does not depend on realism, but on its ability to align with visual action and rhythm.

Stylized Sound Design & Synchresis

Sound redesign of a film scene using non-conventional materials, replacing realistic elements while maintaining strong perceptual coherence.

The project explores the concept of synchresis, demonstrating how even artificial or unrealistic sounds can feel believable when properly aligned with the visual action.

The aim is to challenge the relationship between realism and perception, showing that synchresis depends not on sound realism, but on its integration with movement and on-screen action.
